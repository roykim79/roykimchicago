Writing CSS Is Only As Difficult As You Make It

August 12, 2013

If you're struggling with your CSS, it's probably your fault. We all have the ability to plan ahead but we don't always choose to. As I've been learning to write HTML and CSS, my instructors always made it a point to bring up best practices and it was drilled into my head. Following a good order of operations was one of the topics brought up.

After having an A-HA moment I usually get the itch to code. Everything is in a div, I'm bouncing from my CSS to my HTML to my browser and refreshing after every other line. This is fine when you're playing with it, but as the content grows, the importance of planning ahead becomes more apparent. Your code will be "D.R.Y" and you'll spend less time inspecting elements.

Before firing up sublime, there needs to be some sort of wireframe. The more detailed it is, the easier the rest of the job will be. Next, markup your HTML and make sure all the content is there and in the order you want it. If you're happy with your results go ahead and style it up. The idea is to build everything one layer at a time. It's very unlikely to never have to go back and make changes but the goal is to minimize the number of times this happens. What I'm writing here isn't a secret but as you grow in anything, it can be easy to forget the basics.

A Developing Developer

August 1, 2013

In the past 4 months I've learned much more than I thought I would have. However, of all the things I've learned, learning how to learn was the most important of them all. There's a theory that it takes 10,000 hours to become an expert at somthing. Maybe that number is 6,000, maybe it's 15,000. Regardless, I am nowhere near any of those numbers and I have a long way to go. I'm not going to argue on the actual number but more how it's applied to everybody.

I know I am definitely lucky to have had good instructors groom me from the beginning. I think the habits you form early will shape the rest of your journey to becoming an expert or not. It pains me to watch someone constantly use the mouse and take 10 seconds to get from their editor to the browser when they can hit 2 keys and get there in a second. Now start counting how many times a day you do that and the numbers start adding up. These are small leaks that add up to a lot of waste in the end.

What's the takeaway from this? Well for the 2 people that might actually read this (1 if I exclude myself), in the beginning, focus on best practices. It's much easier to develop a good habit early than to break an old one later. I hate writing blogs but I know it will help me in the end.
