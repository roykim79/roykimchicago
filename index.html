Demo Day Immersion

September 3, 2013

I recently spent a week volunteering for TechStar's demo day. When I made this decision, I had no idea what I was signing up for other than spending a week with one of the best accelerator programs in the country. I figured this was a great opportunity to learn what it takes to make it in this space and get to meet some people who succeeded in the start-up world. I spent the week with TradingView, a fintech company from Russia, helping with their demo day presentation. I didn't write a single line of code or learn a single thing about front-end development, but I took away a great deal of knowledge I find invaluable.

I was looking for a common thread among the founders and the companies they started. The founders all seemed to follow the mantra Steve Jobs made famous in his Stanford commencement speech: "Stay hungry, stay foolish". They treated the company they built like it was their child and no stone was left unturned when looking for ways to make their company better. The drive and dedication they displayed was motivating and showed me the path I chose was not going to be easy.

Being a masochist is only part of the formula to build a successful company, it also takes a big problem and a solution. The common thread I saw was utilizing the power of the internet and all it's data in a meaningful way. The term "Big Data" is everywhere these days and there's a good reason for that. It's true! Data is currency if you know what to do with it. I'm not saying this is a must in order to be successful, but it will definitely be to your advantage to do so. If your company happens to collect a lot of data and does nothing with it, give it to me and I'll use it somehow.

Writing CSS Is Only As Difficult As You Make It

August 12, 2013

If you're struggling with your CSS, it's probably your fault. We all have the ability to plan ahead but we don't always choose to. As I've been learning to write HTML and CSS, my instructors always made it a point to bring up best practices and it was drilled into my head. Following a good order of operations was one of the topics brought up.

After having an A-HA moment I usually get the itch to code. Everything is in a div, I'm bouncing from my CSS to my HTML to my browser and refreshing after every other line. This is fine when you're playing with it, but as the content grows, the importance of planning ahead becomes more apparent. Your code will be "D.R.Y" and you'll spend less time inspecting elements.

Before firing up sublime, there needs to be some sort of wireframe. The more detailed it is, the easier the rest of the job will be. Next, markup your HTML and make sure all the content is there and in the order you want it. If you're happy with your results go ahead and style it up. The idea is to build everything one layer at a time. It's very unlikely to never have to go back and make changes but the goal is to minimize the number of times this happens. What I'm writing here isn't a secret but as you grow in anything, it can be easy to forget the basics.

A Developing Developer

August 1, 2013

In the past 4 months I've learned much more than I thought I would have. However, of all the things I've learned, learning how to learn was the most important of them all. Building a solid foundation early will yield much greater results than if you didn't. There's a theory that it takes 10,000 hours to become an expert at somthing. Maybe that number is 6,000, maybe it's 15,000. Regardless, I am nowhere near any of those numbers and I have a long way to go. I'm not going to argue on the actual number of hours it takes but more on the fact it's applied to everybody.

I was definitely lucky to have had good instructors groom me from the beginning. When coding there are many ways to attack a project but some way are better than others. What I am referring to are best practices. If you take the time to learn them and apply them, those 10,000 hours will be spent building things while learning and not wasted fixing your broken code. If you practice poor CSS, you could end up spending hours looking for a needle in a haystack pulling your hair out at the same time.

So my advice to a developer in development such as myself, learn best practices as soon as possible. You will be saving yourself time and be hours upon hours ahead of your peers.
